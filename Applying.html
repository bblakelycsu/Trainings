How do I put all of this into practice?

Sections 1–4 of this guide summarized the four WCAG 2.2 principles: Perceivable, Operable, Understandable, and Robust. This section helps you apply those ideas in a real CSU course using quick checks, practical steps, and sample workflows.

Use this as a “starter roadmap” for making your course more accessible over time:

  • Do a quick pass before the semester starts.
  • Build small accessibility habits into your weekly routine.
  • Fix the most common issues CTRE sees in courses.
  • Know when and how to ask CTRE and campus partners for support.
Tip:
You do not have to fix everything at once. Pick one small improvement per week (for example, “fix images in Week 1” or “improve headings in my syllabus”) and build from there.

Section 5 – Applying WCAG 2.2 in Your Course

5.1

Pre-Semester Quick Check

Show details

Use this quick check 2–3 weeks before the semester starts to catch major accessibility issues. Focus on high-impact items students will see right away.

High-priority items to review:

  • Syllabus: Uses real headings, lists, and readable fonts; not a scanned image-only PDF.
  • Start Here / Course Overview: Clearly explains how the course is organized and where to begin.
  • Week 1 content: Key documents and pages use headings, lists, and meaningful link text.
  • Videos students will watch early: Have captions (and transcripts for audio-only content).
  • Color & contrast: Main text and headings are easy to read; important messages don’t rely on color alone.
  • Navigation: Syllabus, Grades, and first assignments are easy to locate from the course homepage.
Example workflow:
  1. Open your syllabus in Word and run the built-in Accessibility Checker.
  2. Fix heading structure and alt text suggestions.
  3. Save as a tagged PDF and upload to Brightspace.
  4. Update the “Start Here” module with a short “How this course works” overview.
  5. Check captions on any Week 1 videos and correct critical terminology.
5.2

Weekly Accessibility Habits

Show details

Building small accessibility habits into your weekly prep makes WCAG much more manageable. Think in terms of “what students see this week.”

Try these weekly habits:

  • Headings first: Add clear headings to new Brightspace pages and documents.
  • Alt text as you go: Whenever you add an image, ask “Does this need alt text?” and write it immediately.
  • Captions check: For new recordings, generate captions and quickly fix key terms and names.
  • Consistent structure: Keep each module in the same order (Overview → Materials → Activities → Assessments).
  • Clear instructions: Re-read new assignments and quizzes to ensure expectations are explicit and student-friendly.
Example weekly checklist (10 minutes):
  • “Do all new pages have a Heading 1 title and subheadings where needed?”
  • “Did I add or update alt text for any new images I posted?”
  • “If I posted a new video, did I check captions for accuracy?”
  • “Is this week’s module organized the same way as last week’s?”
  • “Are instructions for new activities clear, including due dates and how to submit?”
5.3

Common Issues & Quick Fixes

Show details

CTRE frequently sees the same accessibility barriers in courses. Here are some high-impact issues and how to fix them quickly.

Frequent issues:

  • Image-only PDFs or slides: Students using screen readers cannot access the text.
  • “Click here” links: Links lack context when read out of order.
  • Color-only feedback: Quizzes and grade markings rely only on red/green.
  • Unstructured pages: Long paragraphs with no headings or lists.
  • Missing or inaccurate captions: Auto-captions misrepresent critical terms.

Quick fixes:

  • Replace scanned PDFs with text-based versions or provide accessible versions alongside scans.
  • Rewrite links so they describe their target (e.g., “Download Week 3 Slides” instead of “Click here”).
  • Add text labels such as “Correct” / “Incorrect” and brief explanations to supplement color feedback.
  • Break long pages into sections with meaningful headings and bullet points.
  • Open key videos in the caption editor and correct important names, terms, and numbers.
Before and after example:

Before: “Click here for the article.”

After: “Read: Smith (2023) – Social Determinants of Health (PDF).”

Impact: Students and screen readers now know exactly what the link leads to, and it’s easier to recognize the resource later.

5.4

Working with Support & Documenting Accommodations

Show details

Accessibility is a shared responsibility across CTRE, Disability Resource Center (DRC), IT, and faculty. You are not expected to solve every technical issue alone.

When to contact CTRE:

  • You’re unsure how to fix an accessibility checker issue in Word, PowerPoint, PDF, or Brightspace.
  • You want a quick review of a course, module, or key assignment for accessibility barriers.
  • You’re adding a new external tool (publisher content, simulations, etc.) and want an accessibility check.

When to coordinate with DRC and other partners:

  • Students share an accommodation letter that affects testing time, note-taking, or formats of materials.
  • You need to provide alternative formats (e.g., captioned video, accessible PDF, alternative assignment).
  • You have questions about how to implement “extended time” or “reduced distraction” environments.

Simple documentation practices:

  • Keep a brief note (for yourself) of accessibility improvements you make each term.
  • Save accessible templates (syllabus, slides, assignment instructions) to reuse in future semesters.
  • Consider adding a short “Accessibility & Support” section in your syllabus and Brightspace “Start Here” module.
Sample “Accessibility & Support” statement for your course page:

“This course is designed with accessibility in mind, following the WCAG 2.2 guidelines and CSU’s accessibility practices. If you have difficulty accessing any course materials (readings, videos, activities, assessments), please contact me and/or CTRE at elearning@csu.edu so we can work together on a solution. Students with accommodation letters from the Disability Resource Center are encouraged to share them early so we can implement your accommodations in a timely way.”
