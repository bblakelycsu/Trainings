What does “Robust” mean?

In WCAG 2.2, “Robust” means your content is built so it works reliably with different browsers, devices, and assistive technologies now and in the future.

For CSU faculty, this often means:

  • Using built-in tools in Brightspace, Word, PowerPoint, and PDF instead of “work-arounds.”
  • Keeping your content clean and structured so screen readers can interpret it correctly.
  • Making sure interactive tools expose clear labels, roles, and status messages.
  • Checking that your materials still work when technology is updated or students use different devices.

Section 4 – Robust (WCAG 2.2)

4.1

Compatible

Show details

WCAG Guideline 4.1 (Compatible) focuses on making content robust enough to be interpreted reliably by a wide variety of user agents and assistive technologies.

What this means:

If your course content is structured cleanly and uses standard tools, screen readers and other technologies can “read” it correctly. If it’s messy, full of images of text, or depends on fragile tricks, students may lose information.

Key practices for your course (tied to WCAG 4.1 success criteria):

A. Clean, well-structured content (Parsing – 4.1.1)

  • Use built-in heading styles, lists, and tables in Word, PowerPoint, Brightspace, and PDF tools.
  • Avoid scanning paper documents as image-only PDFs; use real text-based PDFs whenever possible.
  • When pasting from Word into Brightspace, use “Paste as plain text” and then re-apply headings and lists to avoid messy HTML.
  • Run accessibility checkers in Word, PowerPoint, Acrobat, or Brightspace to catch structural issues.

B. Clear names, roles, and values (Name, Role, Value – 4.1.2)

  • Ensure buttons and links clearly describe their purpose (e.g., “Submit Assignment 1,” “Download Lab Rubric”).
  • When embedding external tools (simulations, forms, videos), include a clear title and description near the embed.
  • Avoid using unlabeled icons alone (e.g., a gear or three dots) for critical actions without nearby text labels.
  • Check that interactive elements (tabs, accordions, activities) can be reached with keyboard focus and have clear labels.

C. Status messages students can perceive (Status Messages – 4.1.3)

  • Make sure important changes are accompanied by clear, visible messages (e.g., “Your quiz attempt has been submitted”).
  • For instructions, tell students what to expect after they click a button (e.g., “After you submit, you will see a confirmation page.”).
  • Avoid relying only on color changes or subtle animations to indicate success or error; add text descriptions too.
  • When you use external tools or forms, verify that they display clear confirmation and error messages.
Example to analyze:
You upload a scanned image of your syllabus as a PDF and link to three external tools. When students click “Submit,” the page just refreshes with no clear message, and screen reader users cannot read the syllabus text at all.
Better approach:
  • Provide a text-based syllabus (Word or tagged PDF) created from an accessible template.
  • Use Brightspace’s built-in assignment, quiz, or discussion tools where possible, so status messages (e.g., “Submission received”) are provided automatically and accessible.
  • For any external tool, add brief instructions and what students should see when their work is submitted successfully.
