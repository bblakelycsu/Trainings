WCAG 2.2 Quick Guide – Section 1: Perceivable

Center for Teaching & Research Excellence · Chicago State University

Overview: What Does “Perceivable” Mean?

Content must be presented in ways that students can perceive it, regardless of disability. This includes text alternatives, captions, adaptable layouts, and clear visual presentation.

Provide text alternatives (“alt text”) for non-text content so it can be changed into large print, braille, speech, symbols, or simpler language.

Best Practices:

  • Describe images meaningfully, not literally.
  • Do not use “image of…” or “picture of…”
  • Decorative images should have empty alt attributes.
Example for Faculty to Analyze:

Is this alt text meaningful? How could it be improved?

Provide captions for video, transcripts for audio, and audio descriptions for essential visuals.

Best Practices:

  • Use human-edited captions for accuracy.
  • Ensure transcripts include speaker identification.
  • Include visual descriptions for essential content in videos.
Example for Faculty to Analyze:

A lecture video includes auto-generated YouTube captions with noticeable errors.

Create content that can be presented in different ways without losing structure.

Best Practices:

  • Use headings (

    –

    ) to structure content.
  • Use list tags instead of manually typing bullet symbols.
  • Avoid using tables for layout.
Example to Analyze:

A syllabus uses bold text and large font to indicate headings instead of actual heading levels.

Make content easy to see and hear, including color contrast, text spacing, and avoiding sensory-only instructions.

Best Practices:

  • Ensure text-to-background contrast ratio of at least 4.5:1.
  • Do not rely on color alone to convey meaning.
  • Provide controls to stop auto-playing media.
Example to Analyze:

A quiz uses red text to indicate incorrect answers and green text to indicate correct ones.
