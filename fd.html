What is ‚Äúaccessible course design‚Äù?

Digital accessibility means all students can independently access and interact with your content, regardless of disability, device, or neurotype.

  • Use clear structure (headings, lists, meaningful links).
  • Provide alternatives (alt text, captions, transcripts).
  • Ensure perceivable color contrast and readable typography.
  • Design for keyboard and assistive technologies.
  • Offer multiple ways to engage, process, and demonstrate learning.
Rule of thumb: If a student can‚Äôt see, hear, use a mouse, process dense text quickly, or attend live sessions, they should still be able to learn and demonstrate mastery in your course.

WCAG 2.2 Map for Teaching POUR framework

WCAG 2.2 organizes accessibility into four principles: content must be Perceivable, Operable, Understandable, and Robust (POUR). Most higher-ed policies target Level AA conformance.

P (Perceivable) O (Operable) U (Understandable) R (Robust)

Perceivable AA

  • Provide text alternatives for non-text content.
  • Offer captions and transcripts for media.
  • Use proper headings & reading order.
  • Maintain sufficient color contrast and scalable text.

Operable AA

  • Everything works via keyboard alone.
  • Give users enough time & avoid flashing content.
  • Provide clear ways to navigate, find content, and know where they are.
  • Make link targets large enough and easy to activate.

Understandable AA

  • Use clear, consistent language and layout.
  • Explain unusual terminology and abbreviations.
  • Keep navigation and controls predictable across pages.
  • Give helpful error messages and instructions for forms.

Robust AA

  • Use HTML and document features as intended.
  • Ensure content works with screen readers and other assistive tech.
  • Avoid relying on one specific browser, device, or plug-in.

Headings, PDFs & Document Accessibility Structure & text

Start with accessible source documents (Word, Google Docs, HTML) and then export to PDF. Use true headings, lists, and styles so assistive technologies can navigate your content.

Good vs. bad headings

Screen readers rely on heading levels to provide an outline view of the document. Visual formatting alone is not enough.

Remediating PDFs

Whenever possible, share HTML or Word instead of PDF. When PDFs are necessary, ensure they are tagged and readable.

  1. Start from an accessible Word/Google Doc with headings, alt text, and lists.
  2. Use Save as PDF or Export, not ‚ÄúPrint to PDF‚Äù.
  3. Run an accessibility checker (Word, Acrobat, or LMS tools).
  4. For scanned PDFs, use OCR and then add tags/headings where possible.
  • You can select and copy text (not just whole page images).
  • Headings appear in the PDF‚Äôs navigation or tags panel.
  • Images have alt text or are marked decorative.
  • Reading order follows the visual layout.
  • There‚Äôs enough color contrast for text and key elements.
Consider replacing non-essential PDFs with:
  • HTML pages in the LMS.
  • Accessible Google Docs or Word files.
  • Short accessible summaries plus the original article via the library.

Media, Color & Link Practices Perceivable & understandable

Multimedia and visual design can support or block learning. Captions, transcripts, color contrast, and descriptive links are core WCAG requirements and central to inclusive pedagogy.

Captions & transcripts

Captions support deaf/hard-of-hearing students, second-language learners, and anyone watching in noisy or quiet settings.

Course Structure & LMS Navigation Operable & predictable

Consistent module structures, clear labeling, and keyboard-friendly navigation support all learners and reduce cognitive load.

Module structure

Align your LMS with a predictable weekly pattern so students quickly know where to look.

  • Use a consistent naming scheme (e.g., ‚ÄúWeek 3 ¬∑ Topic name‚Äù).
  • Order items logically: Overview ‚Üí Materials ‚Üí Activities ‚Üí Assessments ‚Üí Wrap-up.
  • Label required vs. optional materials in text, not just by placement or color.
  • Include brief checklists for complex weeks or projects.
Analyze your course

Pick one module in your LMS. How many clicks does it take for a new student to find:

  • What they are expected to learn this week.
  • What they must do.
  • How they will be assessed.
Keyboard & focus

Many students navigate via keyboard or assistive technologies.

  1. Try tabbing through your course home page.
  2. Confirm that focus outlines are visible and not disabled.
  3. Ensure important buttons/links are reachable without a mouse.
  4. Avoid hover-only disclosures for critical information.

Neurodiversity & Universal Design for Learning Human-centered

Neurodivergent students (e.g., ADHD, autism, dyslexia, anxiety) often encounter barriers that are invisible in traditional course design. Universal Design for Learning (UDL) helps you plan from the start for a wider range of brains.

UDL encourages multiple means of engagement, representation, and action/expression so students can connect with material, understand it, and show what they know in different ways.

Engagement

Make expectations visible

  • Preview weekly tasks and due dates in an overview.
  • Break large projects into smaller milestones.
  • Offer checklists and visual timelines.
Representation

Multiple ways to access content

  • Combine text with visuals, examples, and short videos.
  • Provide slides or outlines in advance, when possible.
  • Offer audio versions or text-to-speech-friendly formats.
Action & Expression

Flexible demonstrations of learning

  • When appropriate, let students choose between formats (e.g., short video, podcast, essay).
  • Give clear rubrics that focus on learning goals, not one ‚Äúright‚Äù format.
  • Allow drafts and feedback cycles to reduce anxiety.
