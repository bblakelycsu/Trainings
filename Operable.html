2. Operable
Need help? Email elearning@csu.edu

What does “Operable” mean?

In WCAG 2.2, “Operable” means that students must be able to navigate and use your course interface, regardless of how they interact with technology.

For CSU faculty, this often means:

  • Ensuring everything can be used with a keyboard (no mouse required).
  • Giving students enough time to complete tasks, quizzes, and activities.
  • Avoiding content that can trigger seizures or strong physical reactions.
  • Providing clear navigation, headings, and links so students don’t get lost.
  • Making interactive controls easy to find, select, and use on different devices.

Section 2 – Operable (WCAG 2.2)

2.1

Keyboard Accessible

Show details

WCAG 2.2 requires that all functionality be usable via a keyboard (or keyboard-like device), without requiring a mouse or complex gestures.

What this means:

Students should be able to move through your content, activate links and buttons, and complete forms using only the keyboard (for example, with the Tab key and Enter).

Key requirements for your course:

  • Check that students can tab through links, buttons, and form fields in a logical order.
  • Make sure there is a visible focus indicator (outline or highlight) when elements are selected.
  • Avoid activities that require dragging with a mouse unless there is a keyboard alternative.
  • Do not trap focus inside widgets or pop-ups; students must be able to tab in and out.
Example to analyze:
You embed a drag-and-drop activity where students must drag labels onto an image with a mouse.
Better approach:
Offer a keyboard-friendly version of the activity (for example, a matching question or multiple-choice quiz) and clearly label it as an equivalent alternative.

2.2

Enough Time

Show details

WCAG 2.2 requires that users have enough time to read and use content. Time limits should be adjustable or removable when possible.

What this means:

Students who read more slowly, use assistive technology, or need breaks must still be able to complete quizzes, forms, and activities without being rushed out unexpectedly.

Key requirements for your course:

  • Avoid very short timers on quizzes and assignments, especially for high-stakes assessments.
  • Work with DRC and department policies to provide extended time accommodations in Brightspace.
  • If a session times out (like a practice quiz), provide a way for students to resume or restart.
  • Warn students before time-limited activities and clearly state the time they will have.
Example to analyze:
A low-stakes review quiz in Brightspace has a 5-minute time limit and automatically submits without warning.
Better approach:
Remove the strict time limit for practice quizzes or extend it significantly, and clearly communicate any time constraints. For exams, coordinate extended-time settings for students with accommodations.

View the official WCAG 2.2 spec for Enough Time

2.3

Seizures & Physical Reactions

Show details

WCAG 2.2 limits content that flashes or moves in ways that can trigger seizures, dizziness, or other strong physical reactions.

What this means:

Avoid flashing, strobing, or rapidly moving content in your course. If something moves or updates automatically, students must be able to pause or stop it.

Key requirements for your course:

  • Do not use content that flashes more than three times per second.
  • Avoid animated GIFs or videos with intense flashing lights or rapidly changing colors.
  • Provide controls to pause, stop, or hide moving content such as carousels or auto-scrolling banners.
  • Be cautious with VR/AR or simulation content that could cause motion sickness; provide alternatives.
Example to analyze:
A “fun” banner GIF on your Brightspace homepage flashes brightly and loops endlessly.
Better approach:
Replace the flashing GIF with a static image or a subtle, slow animation, or remove it entirely. Focus on clear, stable visuals that do not distract or overwhelm students.

View the official WCAG 2.2 spec for Seizures & Physical Reactions

2.4

Navigable

Show details

WCAG 2.2 requires that users can easily find where they are, move around, and locate content and features within a page or site.

What this means:

Your course should have clear headings, consistent menus, and descriptive links so students don’t get lost. Screen reader users should be able to jump quickly to sections and main content.

Key requirements for your course:

  • Use consistent module structures (e.g., Overview, Readings, Lecture, Activities, Assessments).
  • Write descriptive link text (e.g., “Download Week 2 Syllabus” instead of “click here”).
  • Provide clear page titles and headings so students know where they are.
  • Avoid overly long pages without headings; break content into scannable sections.
  • Ensure there is a logical, predictable path to key items like the syllabus, grades, and assignments.
Example to analyze:
A Brightspace module contains several links labeled “Click here” and “More info” without context.
Better approach:
Rewrite links to be descriptive, such as “Watch Week 3 Lecture Video,” “View Week 3 Discussion Instructions,” or “Download Exam 1 Study Guide,” so students and screen readers know exactly where each link goes.

2.5

Input Modalities

Show details

WCAG 2.2 supports multiple ways of interacting with content, including touch, voice, stylus, and assistive technologies—especially on smaller screens.

What this means:

Controls should be easy to tap or click, not overly precise, and should not require complex gestures (like pinch-and-twist) to complete important actions.

Key requirements for your course:

  • Ensure buttons and links are large enough and well spaced for touch screens.
  • Avoid requiring multi-finger or path-based gestures for critical tasks.
  • Make sure labels are clear and located next to the corresponding form fields.
  • Test interactive tools (e.g., simulations, external websites) on mobile and tablet devices.
Example to analyze:
A small “Next” arrow in a mobile-friendly module is hard to tap without zooming in.
Better approach:
Use a larger “Next” button with a clear label and sufficient padding, and avoid placing it too close to other tappable elements to reduce accidental taps.
