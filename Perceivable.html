1. Perceivable

What does “Perceivable” mean?

In WCAG 2.2, “Perceivable” means that students must be able to see, hear, or otherwise access your content. If information is only visual or only audio, some students will miss it.

For CSU faculty, this often means:

  • Providing meaningful alt text for images and graphics.
  • Adding captions and transcripts to video and audio.
  • Using headings, lists, and tables correctly in documents.
  • Ensuring high color contrast and clear visual presentation.

Section 1 – Perceivable (WCAG 2.2)

1.1

Text Alternatives

Show details

WCAG 2.2 requires that non-text content (images, icons, charts, etc.) have a text alternative that serves the same purpose, except for purely decorative content.

What this means (easy to understand):

Anything that isn’t text—such as a picture, icon, or diagram—needs a short written description so that students who cannot see it (or who use a screen reader) still get the important information.

Key requirements for your course:

  • Add alt text to images that carry meaning (e.g., diagrams, photos that teach something).
  • Use empty alt text (alt="") for purely decorative images so screen readers skip them.
  • For images used as buttons or links, describe the action (“Download syllabus”), not the image (“green arrow”).
  • For charts or graphs, give a short alt text plus a longer explanation in the nearby text.
Example to analyze:
A bar chart shows exam averages for three sections. Current alt text: “Chart.”
Better approach:
“Bar chart showing exam averages: Section A 72%, Section B 80%, Section C 88% (highest).”

1.2

Time-based Media (Audio/Video)

Show details

WCAG 2.2 covers prerecorded and live audio/video. Students must have equivalent access through captions, transcripts, or descriptions so that missing sound or visuals doesn’t mean missing content.

What this means (easy to understand):

If you share videos or audio, students who can’t hear well or can’t see the visuals must still be able to understand what you’re teaching.

Key requirements for your course:

  • Provide captions for recorded videos used in your course.
  • Provide a text transcript for audio-only content (podcasts, voice notes).
  • When visuals in a video carry important information (e.g., slides, demonstrations), make sure the audio explains them or provide an additional description.
  • For live online sessions, use live captions when reasonably available, especially for required class sessions.
Example to analyze:
Your recorded lecture uses auto-captions that miss several drug names and dosages.
Better approach:
Open the caption editor in YuJa/Zoom and correct all medical terminology before posting the video as a final resource to students.

View the official WCAG 2.2 spec for Time-based Media

1.3

Adaptable (Structure & Relationships)

Show details

WCAG 2.2 requires that the structure and relationships in your content—like headings, lists, table headers, and labels—can be understood programmatically, not just visually.

What this means (easy to understand):

The way you organize your content on the screen should match the way assistive technologies read it aloud. Visual tricks (bold, spacing) are not enough; you need real headings, lists, and table structure.

Key requirements for your course:

  • Use proper heading styles (Heading 1, 2, 3) instead of just bolding and enlarging text.
  • Use built-in bulleted and numbered lists, not manual dashes or numbers.
  • Set table headers for data tables (e.g., column headings for schedules or grade breakdowns).
  • Ensure reading order makes sense if someone uses a screen reader or keyboard navigation.
  • Do not rely only on visual location (“see the box on the right”); include text descriptions.
Example to analyze:
A syllabus uses bold, large text to label “Week 1,” “Week 2,” etc., but no heading styles were applied in Word.
Better approach:
Apply Heading 2 to each “Week” heading so both readers and assistive technologies can quickly navigate sections.

View the official WCAG 2.2 spec for Adaptable Content

1.4

Distinguishable (Contrast, Color, Visibility)

Show details

WCAG 2.2’s Distinguishable guideline ensures that text and important visual information are easy to see and that meaning is not dependent on color alone.

What this means (easy to understand):

Students must be able to comfortably read your text and understand diagrams and feedback, even if they have low vision or color-vision differences, or if they zoom in on the page.

Key requirements for your course:

  • Do not rely only on color to convey meaning (e.g., red vs. green) — add labels or icons.
  • Choose text and background colors with strong contrast (avoid light gray on white, etc.).
  • Ensure text remains readable when students zoom to at least 200%.
  • Design pages so content reflows and remains usable on smaller screens without horizontal scrolling.
  • Ensure hover or focus pop-ups (like tooltips) are easy to dismiss and don’t cover essential content.
Example to analyze:
Quiz feedback shows correct answers in green and incorrect in red, with no additional labels.
Better approach:
Add text labels such as “Correct” and “Incorrect” next to each answer, and keep the green/red as optional visual reinforcement.
