1. Perceivable

What does “Perceivable” mean for my course?

Students must be able to see, hear, or otherwise access course content. This is especially important for students using assistive technologies, students with low vision, students with hearing disabilities, and neurodivergent learners who depend on predictable formatting.

  • Provide alt text for images and diagrams.
  • Use captions and transcripts for audio and video.
  • Use proper headings and structure in Word, PDFs, and Brightspace.
  • Ensure strong color contrast and do not rely on color alone.

You do not need deep technical skills—most steps involve writing better descriptions or using built-in formatting tools. For more complex adjustments, CTRE and eLearning can help.

Section 1: Perceivable – WCAG 2.2 Guidelines

1.1

Text Alternatives

Show details

Provide meaningful descriptions for images so screen readers can convey the same instructional information to students who cannot see the image.

Example:
A chart shows student performance across the semester.
Bad alt text: “Chart.”

Better alt text: “Line graph showing performance rising from 70% to 88% across 16 weeks.”
1.2

Time-Based Media

Show details

All videos should have captions. Audio-only content should have a transcript. Auto-captions are a good start but usually need editing for accuracy.

Example:
A Zoom recording has auto-generated captions that miss medical terms and student names.

Open the caption editor in YuJa/YouTube/Zoom, correct the errors, and save. If students request it, provide a transcript as well.
1.3

Adaptable Structure

Show details

Use true headings, lists, and tables. Screen readers rely on structure, not just how text looks visually.

Example:
A syllabus uses bold, large text to look like headings, but no actual heading styles.

In Word, apply Heading 1 for course title, Heading 2 for major sections (Policies, Schedule, etc.), and Heading 3 for subsections. Then export to PDF using “Best for electronic distribution and accessibility.”
1.4

Distinguishable Content

Show details

Ensure strong color contrast, reasonable text size, and never use color alone to indicate meaning (such as correct vs. incorrect answers).

Example:
Correct answers are shown in green; incorrect answers are shown in red, with no labels.

Add explicit labels such as “Correct” and “Incorrect,” or use icons (✓ / ✗). Keep color as an extra cue, not the only one.
